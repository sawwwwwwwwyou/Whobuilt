What DCA actually is

DCA = Dollar-Cost Averaging. In plain words: you buy an asset (BTC, stocks, whatever) in equal chunks at some cadence. $100 every Friday, $500 monthly, or on a trigger ("only if price dropped 10% from peak"). You don't time the market — you average over time.

Why now

April 2026: crypto and equities are back at all-time highs. Going in all-at-once is scary; the next move could easily be down. Spreading buys across weeks dilutes the "bought the top" risk. The only question is how to DCA.

What's here

I took 25 DCA strategies — from dumb "buy $461 every Friday" to "buy 4× as much when BTC is down 50% from ATH" — and ran each one through full BTC, ETH, S&P 500, and NASDAQ history. 4 markets × 25 strategies × 2 cadences = 200 cells. You can see what each strategy historically yielded, its drawdown, its risk profile.

How to read the numbers

  • MM (money multiple) — the main number. How many times your entire budget compounded over the period. This is the honest "would I X my money" metric.
  • IRR — annualized return.
  • MaxDD — biggest drawdown on the equity curve. How many times you'd have wanted to quit.
  • Invested — how much the strategy actually deployed. 18% means 82% of the budget sat in cash.

Heads up: if you see "growth on deployed capital" (FPV / invested) somewhere — it's a secondary metric. Value Averaging shows 23.5×, but only because it deployed $41k out of a $224k budget and that $41k happened to grow. That's small-denominator math, not magic. Anchor on MM.

Past performance doesn't predict the future. This is a research project, not financial advice.

How to read this

Before you read the rankings

  1. Test budget. $24k a year × the length of the asset's history. This isn't your deposit. It's a fixed amount so every strategy is compared on equal terms.
  2. Deployed vs idle cash. Some strategies spend the full deposit every period (B1, B2 — plain DCA). Others stockpile cash and release it in waves — for example, «Buy-the-dip» only buys when the market is below its 200-day SMA. Their «Invested» total ends up far below the test budget.
  3. Risk 1-5 is derived from MaxDD: 1 — under 25%, 2 — 25-40%, 3 — 40-55%, 4 — 55-70%, 5 — above 70%. The matcher uses this as your comfort ceiling.
  4. «Budget growth» vs «Invested growth». The first is how the entire plan multiplied — including the cash that sat at 0%. The second is the multiple on dollars that actually reached the market. Cash-buffered strategies see these diverge sharply; for plain DCA they're basically identical.
What's inside

There's no universal «best». Every strategy trades one thing for another

All numbers come from a single backtest run. If a strategy doesn't apply to a market, it's not in the ranking.

01

Returns

Final portfolio value (FPV), annualized IRR, growth on the whole budget, and growth on only the dollars actually invested. They answer two questions: how much the plan returned, and how much of the budget actually worked.

02

Drawdown

MaxDD shows how deep the portfolio fell at its worst point. Often more important than the headline return. It decides whether you'd actually hold the strategy through a real drop.

03

Capital discipline

«Invested» tells you how much money actually reached the asset. Some strategies look great on returns but sit on cash for months waiting for a signal.

04

Context

BTC, ETH, SPY, and NDX behave very differently. Pick the market and cadence first, then compare strategies. Not the other way around.

T10Y2Y (10-year minus 2-year Treasury yield). Inversion historically predicts a recession 12–24 months out. Logic (after ×0.8 tuning): inversion (y<0) — 0.4× (pause + accumulate, wait for the recession). First 18 months after a fresh inversion — 1.6× (deploy buffer, buy the recession bottom). Otherwise — 0.8× (neutral baseline; 20% of each deposit accumulates in the buffer). The sub-1.0× neutral baseline gives the post-inversion 1.6× branch real buffer to deploy — without it the amplify branch clipped to one deposit and the strategy was barely distinguishable from b1. Inversions are rare (4 over the past 27 years on SPY) so the strategy spends most time near baseline. Illustrative signal, not statistically strong because of the small sample. Applies only to SPY/NDX.
