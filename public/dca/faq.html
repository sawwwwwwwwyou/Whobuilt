Basics

8 Q&A

What's actually going on here?

If you want to put money into crypto or stocks for 5+ years and don't want to babysit your account every day — this is the right place.

The idea is simple. Instead of going all-in one day (scary, especially at all-time highs) or trying to time the bottom (no one's good at that) — you buy the asset gradually, in small chunks, on some rule. That's DCA — Dollar-Cost Averaging.

The simplest rule: every Friday, buy $100 worth of Bitcoin. That's it. Don't look at the price, don't read the news, don't try to time anything.

You can be smarter though. For example: "normally I buy $100, but if Bitcoin is down 30% from peak — I buy $300". Cheaper accumulation on corrections. For this to work, you have to save cash in calm times — otherwise there's nothing to fire when the drop comes.

Here you'll find 25 such rules. All tested on the full price history of Bitcoin, Ethereum, S&P 500, and NASDAQ. You can see what each rule historically returned and at what drawdown. Compare and pick the one that fits you and your nerves.

Why DCA instead of "go all-in and forget"?

As of April 2026, crypto and equities are again at the highest prices in their history (called ATH — all-time high). If you go in all-at-once now, there's a real chance of buying right at the peak before a -30% correction. Psychologically that's hell: you'll spend a year or two staring at red numbers and second-guessing yourself.

DCA dilutes that risk. Some money goes in now at high prices, some on later corrections at lower prices. Your average buy price ends up moderate.

The downside: if the market keeps ripping up from here without stopping, you'll earn less than if you'd gone all-in. That's the price of "didn't buy the very top". Most people are happy to pay it.

Who is this for?

DCA is right for you if you:

  • Can put money in for 5+ years. Short horizons don't suit DCA.
  • Don't want to read news every day and time the market.
  • Understand -50% drawdowns happen and won't sell during them.
  • Can stay calm seeing red for a year or two straight.

DCA is not for you if you:

  • Want to double your money in six months (wrong place).
  • Need the money in the next year (risk of selling at a low).
  • Can't emotionally handle -50% on your portfolio — pick a less volatile asset instead.

What does ATH mean?

All-time high. The highest price the asset has ever reached.

April 2026 BTC ATH ≈ $109k, S&P 500 ATH ≈ 6 100. When people say "the market is at ATH", they mean it's worth more right now than ever before.

"At ATH" doesn't itself mean "will fall tomorrow" — markets often spend years at ATH, repeatedly making new ones. But it does mean buying right now isn't cheap, and a drop after ATH stings emotionally more than a drop "from the middle".

What's a drawdown?

How much your portfolio fell from its peak. You hit $100k, the portfolio drops to $40k — drawdown is 60%.

It's an emotional metric — how painful would it have been to hold the strategy and not panic-sell. On crypto, 70-80% drawdowns happen regularly (every cycle). On stocks >50% is once-in-10-15-years (e.g., 2008).

The MaxDD column is the biggest drawdown over the whole test period. MaxDD = 76% means at the worst moment you'd have seen the portfolio down three quarters from its peak. If that's unacceptable to you — don't pick that strategy.

Weekly or monthly — which one?

Just buy frequency:

  • Weekly: $461.54 every Friday.
  • Monthly: $2,000 on the first Friday of each month.

Same total per year — $24k. Easier to top up your broker once a month and forget. Weekly is technically a bit more responsive to drops, but the long-run difference is barely there. Pick what's easier for you to actually do.

Why do strategies save cash?

Many "smart" strategies don't spend the whole budget every week. They stash part in a buffer and fire it on big drawdowns.

The logic: in calm times, buy $200; save the other $260. When Bitcoin drops 50% — the strategy spends accumulated cash in one big buy at the cheap price.

For this to work, you need "extra" cash sitting in your account. If you want the whole budget spent right away — pick simple DCA (B1 or B2), no saving.

Cap is the buffer's ceiling ($12,000 by default). Anything above sits idle and returns at par at the end. Models reality: you can't accumulate forever.

What's lump-sum?

Lump-sum = "go in with everything on day one". If you have $24k and you put it all into Bitcoin in a single day — that's lump-sum. DCA is the opposite — gradual.

Statistically lump-sum beats DCA in 2 cases out of 3. Markets rise more than they fall, so earlier-in = more time to grow.

But in 1 out of 3 cases lump-sum is a disaster: you bought the peak right before a crash. E.g., putting everything into Bitcoin at the November 2021 peak ($69k) meant -78% by end of 2022.

DCA is the compromise. Less profit in the perfect scenario, but never "I bought the top".

The B3 (lump at start) strategy in the table shows what lump-sum would have done historically — for comparison.

Metrics

9 Q&A

What is FPV?

Final Portfolio Value — how much money you'd have at the end of the test period. "What you'd really be holding".

Three parts: asset bought × end price + cash in buffer + cash that overflowed the buffer.

What is MM (money multiple)?

The main number for comparing strategies. How many times your entire budget compounded over the period.

You put in $24k, you ended with $200k → MM = 8.33×.

This is the most honest "how many X did I make" metric, because it's against the full budget — not just what got deployed into the asset.

When comparing strategies — anchor on MM first.

What is IRR?

Annualized return. IRR = 30% means your money grew at an effective 30% per year over the whole period.

Equivalent to "annual percentage", but accounts for the fact that you deposited gradually rather than as a lump.

What is MaxDD?

Maximum drawdown over the whole test period. If at some point the portfolio lost 76% from its peak, that's MaxDD = 76%.

About emotional risk, not final return. Helps you judge "how painful would this have been to hold without selling".

What's IRR/MaxDD?

Return divided by drawdown. Risk-adjusted return.

The idea: a strategy at 50% annualized with 80% drawdown isn't better than 25% with 25% drawdown — same "profit per unit of pain". Higher IRR/MaxDD = more comfortable to hold.

What is Invested?

How much money the strategy actually spent on the asset over the period.

If the budget was $224k and Invested = $87k, the strategy bought $87k worth of the asset. The other $137k sat in cash or got returned at par.

Context, not victory. Low Invested + high MM = "didn't deploy much, what I did grew well". High Invested + low MM = "deployed everything, didn't grow".

What about "growth on deployed capital" — and why not trust it?

It's a secondary metric — growth of only the actually-deployed portion. Can be very misleading.

Example. Value Averaging on Bitcoin shows growth-on-deployed = 23.5×. Sounds magical. But that's because the strategy only deployed $41k of a $224k budget. Those $41k grew to $964k. The other $183k sat in cash the whole time.

Against the full budget, the same strategy gives MM = 4.30×. So $224k turned into $964k — normal growth, not magic.

Small-denominator effect. Divide by a small number, any growth looks huge. So anchor on MM, not on FPV/invested.

What's Risk 1-5?

Simplified risk category derived from MaxDD:

  • 1: less than 25% drawdown (comfortable)
  • 2: 25-40% (tolerable)
  • 3: 40-55% (nerve-testing)
  • 4: 55-70% (crypto-survivor)
  • 5: 70%+ (only for those who can stomach "everything's gone")

Quick way to filter out strategies that definitely don't fit. For real choices, look at MaxDD percentage directly.

What is Forgone?

Money the strategy couldn't spend because the buffer overflowed (above the $12k cap).

This cash returns to you at par at the end — no growth, like under the mattress. It counts in FPV (you really get it back), but it didn't grow in the asset.

High forgone = the strategy frequently couldn't spend its accumulated cash, and a big chunk of the budget didn't actually "work".

Strategies

19 Q&A

B1: Pure DCA — what does it do?

The simplest possible strategy. $461.54 into the asset every week. No conditions, no signals, no savings.

On the long run with a rising asset, often beats many "smart" strategies — it doesn't miss growth waiting for a drop. The baseline reference: if a smart strategy can't beat B1, the complexity wasn't worth it.

If this is your first time DCA-ing — start here. Simple works.

B2: Monthly Lump — what does it do?

Same as B1 but rarer: $2,000 on the first Friday of each month, no buys on other days. Same $24k a year.

Logistically easier — one transfer to the broker per month vs four. Results almost identical to B1, differs in the third decimal.

B3: Lump at start — what does it do?

Goes in with the entire budget ($24k × number of years in the period) on day one and holds to the end.

The lump-sum reference — "what would I have made if I could deploy everything at once". On rising markets it beats every DCA strategy. But for most people it's unrealistic — you don't have $216k of free cash to deploy in one day. A theoretical ceiling, not a plan.

Convex / Linear / Tiered — what's the difference?

All three families say "buy more on drawdowns". They differ in the curve shape — how much exactly to buy at what drawdown depth:

  • Linear: buy size grows in direct proportion. Price down 25% → buy 1.5×. Down 50% → buy 2×.
  • Convex: convex curve. Barely reacts to small drops, fires aggressively on big ones. Idea: deep drawdowns are the best entries.
  • Tiered: stairs. Drawdown <30% → 1×, 30-50% → 2×, 50-70% → 4×, 70%+ → 5×. Discrete version of convex.

All three save cash in a buffer so they have something to spend when the signal triggers.

Concave — opposite of convex?

Yes. Convex says "buy more on big drawdowns". Concave says "buy more on small drawdowns, don't ramp further".

Idea: mean reversion (the bounce after a move) is strongest on small 5-10% pullbacks. Big drawdowns are structural crisis territory, and adding more there is dangerous.

On bull markets concave does well, on bear markets it loses to convex.

52w vs ATH — what's the difference?

Different ways to measure "how much the asset is currently down":

  • 52w: from the high of the last year. Resets every year.
  • ATH: from the historical high across all time.

Example. Bitcoin ATH was $69k in November 2021. Dropped to $15k in 2022. By mid-2024, the 52w metric says BTC is no longer "in drawdown" (recent year had a higher peak). The ATH metric still sees a drawdown from 2021's top.

Which is better is open. ATH has a longer memory and is more cautious; 52w is more reactive.

Value Averaging — how does it work?

The strategy sets a target growth trajectory for the portfolio. E.g., "by year-end I want $24k in portfolio".

Each week it asks — is the current portfolio below the target line, or above it?

  • Below: top up enough to catch up.
  • Above: don't buy, let cash accumulate.

The Bitcoin paradox: the strategy buys little early, BTC ramps hard, and then the strategy doesn't buy for years (portfolio is already above the target line). End result: ~18% of the budget actually deployed. So MM ends up modest, even though "growth on deployed" looks crazy good (see the FPV/invested question).

Above-SMA200 / Below-SMA200 — what do they do?

SMA200 = the asset's average price over the last 200 days. Classic trend indicator: above SMA200 → "uptrend", below → "downtrend".

  • Above-SMA200 only: buy only when price is above SMA200. I.e., only in an uptrend. Don't catch the falling knife.
  • Below-SMA200 only: buy only when price is below SMA200. Contrarian — buy only on corrections.
  • Below-SMA200 aggressive: same as Below, but spends the entire accumulated buffer on each such bar with no per-bar cap.

On strong bull markets Above wins; on bear, Below.

Bi-modal (core + reserve) — what does it do?

A strategy with two buckets:

  • Core (50% of deposit): goes into the asset every week as regular DCA.
  • Reserve (50% of deposit): accumulates separately, waits for catastrophe. Activates only when drawdown exceeds threshold (60% for Bitcoin, 15% for S&P) — at which point the entire reserve is deployed at once.

Idea: half the budget is always working, the other half waits for a big correction and buys the bottom.

Vol-scaled — what does it do?

Buys inversely proportional to volatility. The calmer the market, the more; the crazier, the less.

Idea: high volatility = unpredictability, better to wait. Low volatility = trend is steady, can add. Size auto-normalizes so on average exactly $461 a week is spent.

WoW-linear — what does it do?

WoW = Week-over-Week. Simple short-term contrarian:

  • Last week price dropped 5% → buy 1.5×.
  • Last week price rose → buy 0.5×.

Doesn't try to read "big" drawdowns, only reacts to the latest move. Catches small pullbacks well.

Momentum reversion — what does it do?

"Last week red — buy more, last week green — buy less". Same idea as WoW-linear but with fixed dollar amounts: $500 / $300 / $200 depending on the sign.

Loses on sustained trends (doesn't ride the move), wins on chop.

Below-200WMA only — what does it do?

Same as Below-SMA200, but the threshold is the 200-week weighted average (not 200-day).

A strong "Bitcoin is cheap" indicator — historically Bitcoin rarely spends time below its 200-week average, and those moments coincide with the best buying opportunities.

The strategy saves cash for a long time (most of the time Bitcoin is above 200WMA), then dumps it all into Bitcoin when the price drops below. Visually: this happens rarely, every 2-4 years.

Halving cycle aware — what does it do?

Bitcoin halving — every ~4 years, miner reward is cut in half. Historically tied to the price cycle: post-halving comes a bull run (12-18 months), then top, then bear, then recovery before the next halving.

The strategy varies buy size by months since the last halving:

  • 0-6 months: 1× (early, accumulate)
  • 6-18 months: 1.5× (bull start, lean in)
  • 18-30 months: 0.5× (top zone, careful)
  • 30-48 months: 2× (bear, cheap prices)

Bitcoin only (other assets don't have halvings).

Pi Cycle Top avoidance — what does it do?

Pi Cycle Top — an indicator that historically called Bitcoin tops very accurately. When the 111-day moving average crosses above the 350-day MA × 2 — Bitcoin is usually near a cycle top.

Strategy: while no signal, runs as regular DCA. When the signal fires — pause for 4 weeks, no buys. Defends against buying right at the peak.

Over the 9-year test, the signal triggered 2-3 times, and each time was indeed right before a correction.

MVRV / NUPL / Mayer Multiple — what are they?

All three are crypto indicators showing "is it expensive right now":

  • MVRV Z-Score: how overvalued Bitcoin is vs "fair" price. Z < 0 → cheap, Z > 7 → bubble.
  • NUPL: share of holders currently in profit. NUPL ≥ 0.75 → euphoria (top), NUPL < 0 → panic (bottom).
  • Mayer Multiple: price / 200-day moving average. < 0.8 → cheap, > 2.4 → expensive.

All three are used as multipliers: cheaper indicator = bigger buy. BTC and ETH only — stocks don't have these metrics.

200WMA Heatmap — what does it do?

Like Mayer Multiple but using the 200-week moving average. On popular sites this is rendered as a color heatmap (hence "heatmap" in the name).

Ratio r = price / 200WMA:

  • r < 1 → 3× (cheap)
  • 1.0-1.5 → 2×
  • 1.5-3.0 → 1×
  • 3.0-5.0 → 0.5× (expensive)
  • > 5.0 → 0× (bubble, no buys)

ETH/BTC ratio / Merge regime — what are they?

Both ETH-only.

ETH/BTC ratio gated: buys more ETH when it's cheap relative to Bitcoin. Compares the current ETH/BTC ratio with its 200-week median.

Merge regime: separate calibration before and after The Merge (September 15, 2022, ETH transition to Proof-of-Stake). Pre and post are different market regimes with different thresholds.

VIX / CAPE / Yield curve — what are they?

All three are macro indicators for stocks:

  • VIX-gated: VIX is "the fear index". High VIX = market panic = more buying (fear = opportunity). VIX < 15 → 0.4×, VIX > 40 → 2×.
  • CAPE-gated: Shiller CAPE = "10-year P/E", a classic market valuation indicator. CAPE < 15 → cheap (like 2009), > 45 → bubble (like dotcom 2000).
  • Yield curve aware: yield curve inversion (10-year bonds yielding less than 2-year) historically predicts recessions 12-18 months out. The strategy is cautious during the inversion itself, but actively buys during the 18 months after (market usually cheap then).

Methodology

6 Q&A

Where does the data come from?

Prices: CoinMetrics for Bitcoin and Ethereum (open source, daily data since 2010). Yahoo Finance for S&P 500 (since 1957) and NASDAQ-100 (since 1985).

On-chain metrics (MVRV, NUPL): also CoinMetrics.

Macro: FRED (Federal Reserve) for VIX and yield curve. multpl.com for Shiller CAPE.

All data is public and free.

What does snapshot date 2026-04-24 mean?

The last date through which data is loaded. All backtests end here. If it looks "stale" — that's normal, all numbers on the page correspond to this moment.

To update, data has to be re-pulled and the matrix recomputed. Manual operation, not automatic.

What's the test period?

Depends on the market:

  • BTC: from 2017-01-01 to today (~9 years). Covers 2 full halving cycles.
  • ETH: from 2018-01-13 to today (~8 years).
  • SPY (S&P 500): from 1999-03-10 to today (~27 years). Includes dotcom 2000, GFC 2008, COVID 2020, 2022 bear.
  • NDX (NASDAQ-100): from 1999-03-10 to today (~27 years).

How is IRR computed?

Newton's method finds the annual rate at which the sum of all your contributions equals the final portfolio value. Computed at weekly frequency, then raised to the 52nd power for the annual equivalent.

In plain words: "average annual return given that you contributed gradually".

Can a backtest like this be trusted?

With caveats — yes.

What's honest about it:

  • Only information available at the moment of decision is used. The strategy doesn't "know the future".
  • Same engine as the real bot. No tweaks against history.
  • Metrics computed standardly.
  • Every dollar accounted for: budget = spent + cash + returned.

What's not included: broker fees (typically 0.1-1%), spread (real for crypto), taxes, slippage on large volumes. These will eat 1-3% of your real annual return. Not a catastrophe, but be aware.

Maybe the strategies were picked by hindsight?

Good question. This is called data snooping — when someone tests 1,000 strategies, picks top-3, and says "these are great". In reality it's just noise, and over the next 9 years those top-3 may turn out average.

Here, 25 strategies were taken from public ideas (classic DCA, Mayer Multiple, Pi Cycle, MVRV — all from textbooks and crypto blogs). No one calibrated parameters against the current data. Multipliers and thresholds are mostly "round numbers from the spec".

Even so — out-of-sample testing on the next 9 years would show different numbers. Past doesn't predict the future. This is a "what would have happened" study, not a "what will happen" forecast.

Practice

5 Q&A

Is this financial advice?

No. This is a research project.

I'm not a licensed financial advisor, don't know your circumstances, and bear no responsibility for your decisions. Make your own conclusions. If you plan to invest meaningful money — talk to a real advisor.

How do I pick a strategy from 25?

Steps:

  1. Set your risk. What max drawdown can you emotionally handle? If 30% is the ceiling, ignore strategies with MaxDD > 30%.
  2. From the remainder, take top-3 by MM (the primary metric).
  3. Read each one's description. If you don't get what the strategy does — don't pick it.
  4. Among finalists, pick the one whose description you can explain to a friend in 30 seconds. Complex strategies don't survive contact with reality.

The main page has "Pick by personality" — an interactive filter that narrows the choice with 4-5 simple questions. Useful if the matrix is overwhelming.

What's a meaningful DCA timeframe?

Minimum: 6-12 months. Less is no longer DCA, it's market-timing in small steps.

Sweet spot: 2-4 years. On crypto, you should at least see one halving cycle. On stocks — survive at least one correction.

Long-term: 5-10+ years. Then strategy choice matters less — period length eats the difference between "smart" and "dumb".

What separates real DCA from a simulation?

Mainly — discipline. In a simulation the strategy buys every Friday no matter what. In real life you'll skip a Friday because of work, forget, or be too scared to buy during a crash. Most of the real-vs-backtest gap comes from this.

Second: fees and spread. Each trade loses 0.1-1%, over the long run that eats 1-3% of annual return.

Third: taxes. In most countries selling crypto or stocks is taxable. The simulation computes gross (pre-tax), reality is net.

I don't get any of this. What do I do?

If this is your first DCA — pick B1 (Pure DCA weekly) or B2 (Monthly lump). Just buy a fixed amount on a regular schedule. Don't try to wrap your head around MVRV or Pi Cycle on day one.

"Smart" strategies only work if you understand why they're smart. Otherwise you'll miss a signal, fail to react to a drawdown, break everything.

B1 on Bitcoin over 9 years yielded MM ~ 8.5×. Not perfect (B3 lump was 87×), but honest and without emotional swings.

Past performance doesn't predict the future. This is a research project, not financial advice.
