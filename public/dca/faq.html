Basics

What is DCA?

DCA = Dollar-Cost Averaging. In plain words: you buy an asset (BTC, stocks, ETF) in equal chunks at a regular cadence.

Example: every Friday you top up your brokerage with $100 and immediately buy BTC for that hundred. That's it. No timing the bottom, no waiting for the perfect moment — you just enter at equal time-spaced steps.

The idea: if the market dropped, your $100 buys more BTC. If it rose, less. Over the long run the price averages out and you don't depend on luck to catch the top or bottom.

Why does DCA matter right now?

As of April 2026, BTC, ETH, S&P 500, and NASDAQ are all back at all-time highs — most expensive in history.

If you go in all-at-once, there's a real chance you bought the peak right before a -30% correction — and that's a mental hellscape. Spreading buys over 6-12 months dilutes the "bought the top" risk: part of the budget will land on corrections at lower prices.

DCA doesn't make you richer in the perfect scenario (lump-sum wins there). It defends against the worst scenario — and at ATH the worst scenario is what scares people most.

What's ATH?

ATH = All-Time High. The price the asset has never exceeded historically.

BTC ATH in April 2026 ≈ $109k. S&P 500 ATH ≈ 6 100. When people say "the market is at ATH", they mean it's worth more right now than ever before.

What is drawdown?

Drawdown = how much your portfolio fell from its peak. If you peaked at $100k and the portfolio dropped to $40k, drawdown is 60%.

It's an emotional metric: how painful it would have been to hold the strategy. On crypto, 70-80% drawdowns happen regularly. On stocks >50% is rare (and usually means 2008).

MaxDD in the table is the maximum drawdown across the whole test period. MaxDD = 76% means at the worst moment of the period the strategy lost three quarters of its peak value before recovering.

What's a cadence — weekly or monthly?

Cadence = how often the strategy makes decisions and deploys cash.

  • Weekly: $461.54 every Friday = $24k a year.
  • Monthly: $2,000 on the first Friday of the month = same $24k.

Same budget, different frequency. Monthly is easier psychologically (one transfer per month and forget). Weekly is technically more responsive (faster reaction to drops). Difference over the long run is small.

What are buffer and cap?

Many "smart" strategies don't spend everything every week — they save cash to fire on drawdowns. That saved cash is the buffer (or pool).

Cap limits buffer size: $12,000 by default. Anything above is treated as "forgone" — sits idle and returns at par at the end.

This simulates reality: you can't accumulate cash forever; at some point you either spend it or it just sits there.

How does DCA differ from lump-sum?

Lump-sum = "go all-in at once". Instead of $24k a year as $461 weekly, you put $24k in on day one.

Statistically, lump-sum beats DCA in ~2/3 of historical periods — markets rise more than they fall, so earlier-in = more time to grow. But in 1/3 of cases lump-sum is a disaster: you bought the peak right before a correction.

DCA is the compromise: lower expected return, lower risk of "I bought the top".

The B3 (lump at start) strategy in the table shows the reference lump-sum return for comparison.

Metrics

What is FPV?

FPV = Final Portfolio Value at the end of the period.

It's three parts: asset price × shares accumulated + idle cash in the buffer + forgone funds (cash that overflowed the cap and returns at par).

The amount you'd actually have at the end.

What is MM (money multiple)?

MM = FPV / total_deposited. How many times your entire budget compounded.

If over the period you deposited $24k and ended up with $200k, MM = 8.33×. This is the most honest "would I X my money" metric — denominated against the full budget, not just what got deployed into the asset.

This is the primary metric on the page. When comparing strategies, anchor on MM.

What is IRR?

IRR = Internal Rate of Return, annualized.

If IRR = 30%, your money grew at an effective 30% per year over the whole period. It's the "annual percentage" equivalent that accounts for the fact that you didn't deposit a lump but rather a stream over time.

Computed by solving for the rate that makes the NPV of cashflows equal to zero (Newton's method).

What is MaxDD?

MaxDD = Maximum Drawdown of the equity curve.

For each moment in time, look at the highest the portfolio reached previously and compare to the current. The biggest drop is MaxDD.

MaxDD = 76% means at the worst point of the period you'd have seen the portfolio down three quarters from its peak. About emotional risk, not final return.

What's IRR/MaxDD?

Risk-adjusted return: annual return divided by max drawdown.

The idea: a strategy at IRR 50% with 80% drawdown isn't better than IRR 25% with 25% drawdown — same "return per unit of pain".

Higher IRR/MaxDD = more comfortable to hold. There's a separate ranking by this metric on the page.

What is Invested?

Invested = how much money the strategy actually spent on the asset over the period.

If the budget was $224k and Invested = $87k, the strategy bought only $87k worth of asset; the remaining $137k sat in cash or was forgone.

Context, not victory. Low Invested + high MM = "I didn't deploy much, but what I did grew well". High Invested + low MM = "I deployed everything but it didn't grow".

What does "growth on deployed capital" (FPV / invested) mean?

Secondary metric: growth of only the actually-deployed portion. Can be very misleading.

Example: Value Averaging on BTC shows FPV/invested = 23.5×. Sounds like magic. But that's because the strategy only deployed $41k of a $224k budget. Those $41k grew to $964k. The other $183k sat in cash the whole time.

Against the full budget the same strategy gives MM = 4.30× — i.e., $224k turned into $964k. Not magic, just normal growth.

Small-denominator effect: divide by a small number, any growth looks huge. That's why MM-on-gross is the primary metric here.

What does Risk 1-5 mean?

A simplified risk category derived from MaxDD:

  • 1: MaxDD < 25% (comfortable)
  • 2: 25-40%
  • 3: 40-55%
  • 4: 55-70%
  • 5: 70%+ (crypto-survivor only)

Quick navigation. For real choices, look at MaxDD percentage directly.

What is Forgone?

Forgone = money the strategy couldn't spend because the buffer overflowed (above the $12k cap).

These dollars return to the investor at par at the end — no growth, like under the mattress. Counted in FPV (the investor really gets them back) but with zero chance to grow.

High forgone = strategy frequently couldn't spend its accumulated cash, and a big chunk of the budget didn't "work" in the asset.

Strategies

B1: Pure DCA — what is it?

The simplest strategy possible. Every week $461.54 goes into the asset. No conditions, no savings, no signals.

This is the baseline: if a smart strategy can't beat B1, the added complexity wasn't worth it. On the long run with a rising asset B1 beats many — it doesn't miss growth waiting for a drawdown.

B2: Monthly Lump — what is it?

Same total as B1, but you buy on the first Friday of each month — $2,000 at once instead of $461 weekly.

Logistically easier (one transfer a month). Results almost identical to B1, differs in the third decimal. Choose by preference, not by return.

B3: Lump at start — what is it?

You go in with the entire budget ($24k × number of years) on day one and hold to the end.

Lump-sum reference: "what would I have made if I could deploy everything at once". On rising markets B3 beats every DCA strategy (earlier in = more time to grow). At ATH followed by a correction B3 also gives maximum pain.

In reality most people don't have $24k × 9 years = $216k of free cash to deploy on day one. So B3 is a theoretical ceiling, not a real plan.

Convex / Linear / Tiered — what's the difference?

All three are "buy more on drawdowns" strategies. They differ in the shape of the curve: how much exactly to buy at drawdown X%.

  • Linear: buy size grows in direct proportion to drawdown depth. -25% → 1.5×. -50% → 2×.
  • Convex: convex, accelerating curve. Barely reacts to small drops, fires hard on deep ones. Hypothesis: deep drawdowns are the best entry moments.
  • Tiered: stairs. DD <30% → 1×, 30-50% → 2×, 50-70% → 4×, 70+% → 5×. Discrete version of convex.

All three accumulate a buffer so they have something to spend when the signal fires.

Concave — opposite of convex?

Yes. Convex says "buy on big drawdowns", concave says "buy on small drawdowns, don't ramp further".

Hypothesis: mean reversion is strongest on small pullbacks (5-10%), and big drawdowns are structural crisis, not justified buys.

On bull markets concave does well — small pullbacks correct quickly. On bear markets it loses to convex.

52w vs ATH — which is better?

It's what reference price drawdown is measured from:

  • 52w: from the high of the last 52 weeks (year). Drawdown resets each year.
  • ATH: from the all-time historical high. Drawdown stays "deep" until the price exceeds the old ATH.

On crypto after a big bear market, an ATH-strategy will keep seeing "60% drawdown" for years, until BTC exceeds the old peak. A 52w-strategy only looks back a year — drawdown could be 0% even with the ATH far away.

Value Averaging — how does it work?

Idea: you set a target growth trajectory for the portfolio. E.g., "by end of year I want $24k in portfolio".

Each week: is the current portfolio below the target line, or above?

  • Below: top up to catch up. If the market dropped, you'll need to top up more.
  • Above: don't buy, let the buffer accumulate.

The paradox: on BTC the strategy buys little early, BTC goes up massively, and after that the strategy doesn't buy for years (portfolio is already above the line). End result: $41k actually deployed out of $224k budget.

Above-SMA200 / Below-SMA200 — what are they?

SMA200 = average price over the last 200 days. Classic trend indicator.

  • Above-SMA200 only: buy only when price is ABOVE the 200-day average. I.e., only in an uptrend. Don't catch the falling knife.
  • Below-SMA200 only: buy only when price is BELOW the 200-day average. Contrarian — only buy on corrections.
  • Below-SMA200 aggressive: same as above, but spends the ENTIRE accumulated buffer on each below-SMA200 bar instead of being capped per-bar.

On strong bull markets above wins; on bear, below.

Bi-modal (core + reserve) — what is it?

A strategy with two buckets:

  • Core (50% of deposit): goes into the asset every week as pure DCA.
  • Reserve (50% of deposit): accumulates separately. Activates ONLY when drawdown crosses a threshold (60% for BTC, 15% for SPY) — at which point the entire reserve is deployed at once.

Idea: half the budget always works, the other half waits for a catastrophe and buys the bottom.

Vol-scaled — what is it?

Buy inversely proportional to volatility: the calmer the market, the more; the crazier, the less.

Hypothesis: high volatility = unpredictability, better to wait. Low volatility = trend is steady, can add. Size auto-normalizes so on average exactly $461 a week is spent.

WoW-linear — what is it?

WoW = Week-over-Week. If price dropped 5% last week, buy 1.5×. If it rose, buy 0.5×.

A simple short-term contrarian strategy. Catches small pullbacks well, but doesn't try to read "big" drawdowns — only reacts to the latest move.

Momentum reversion — what is it?

"Last week red — buy more, last week green — buy less."

Same idea as WoW-linear but with fixed dollar amounts: $500 / $300 / $200 depending on the sign. Loses on long trends (doesn't ride growth), wins on chop.

Below-200WMA only — what is it?

Same as Below-SMA200, but the threshold is the weighted 200-week moving average (200WMA). A strong "BTC is cheap" indicator — historically BTC rarely spends time below its 200WMA, and those periods coincide with the best buying opportunities.

The strategy accumulates cash for a long time, then pours it into BTC at once when the price drops below 200WMA. Visually clear on the chart that this happens rarely (once every 2-4 years).

Halving cycle aware — what is it?

BTC halving — an event every ~4 years when miner reward is cut in half. Historically tied to the price cycle: post-halving comes a bull run (~12-18 months), then top, then bear, then recovery before the next halving.

The strategy varies buy size based on months since the last halving:

  • 0-6 months: 1× (early, accumulate)
  • 6-18 months: 1.5× (bull start)
  • 18-30 months: 0.5× (top zone, careful)
  • 30-48 months: 2× (bear, cheap prices)

Pi Cycle Top avoidance — what is it?

Pi Cycle Top — an indicator that historically very accurately called BTC tops: when the 111-day MA crosses above the 350-day MA × 2, BTC is usually near the cycle top.

Strategy: while no signal, runs as pure DCA. When the signal fires — pause for 4 weeks (don't buy). Defends against buying right at the peak.

On BTC stress_b over 9 years the signal triggered 2-3 times, and each time really was right before a correction.

MVRV / NUPL / Mayer Multiple — what are they?

All three are on-chain metrics for crypto, computed from blockchain data.

  • MVRV Z-Score: how overvalued BTC is vs "fair" price. Z < 0 → capitulation, Z > 7 → bubble.
  • NUPL: share of holders in profit. NUPL ≥ 0.75 → euphoria (top), NUPL < 0 → panic (bottom).
  • Mayer Multiple: price / 200-day MA. MM < 0.8 → "cheap", MM > 2.4 → "expensive".

All three are used as multipliers: the "cheaper" the indicator, the bigger the buy. BTC and ETH only.

200WMA Heatmap — what is it?

Like Mayer Multiple but using the 200-week weighted moving average. Bitcoin Investor Tool on popular sites paints this as a heatmap.

r = price / 200WMA. r < 1 → 3× (cheap), 1.0-1.5 → 2×, 1.5-3.0 → 1×, 3.0-5.0 → 0.5× (expensive), > 5.0 → 0× (bubble, no buys).

ETH/BTC ratio / Merge regime — what are they?

ETH/BTC ratio gated (ETH only): buy more ETH when it's cheap relative to BTC. Compares ETH/BTC ratio to its 200-week median.

Merge regime (ETH only): a separate calibration before and after The Merge (Sep 15, 2022, ETH transition to Proof-of-Stake). Pre-Merge and post-Merge are different market regimes with different Mayer thresholds.

VIX / CAPE / Yield curve — what are they?

All three are macro indicators for stocks.

  • VIX-gated: VIX (volatility index) → market panic. High VIX → more buying (fear = opportunity). VIX < 15 → calm → 0.4×; VIX > 40 → panic → 2×.
  • CAPE-gated: Shiller CAPE = "P/E over 10 years". CAPE < 15 → cheap (like 2009), > 45 → bubble (like dotcom 2000). Multipliers 1.6× / 1.0× / 0.8× / 0.4× / 0.2×.
  • Yield curve aware: yield curve inversion (10y < 2y) historically predicts recessions 12-18 months out. The strategy is cautious during inversion but actively buys during the 18 months after (market usually cheap).

Methodology

Where does the data come from?

Prices: CoinMetrics for BTC and ETH (open source, daily data since 2010). Yahoo Finance for S&P 500 (since 1957) and NASDAQ-100 (since 1985).

On-chain metrics: also CoinMetrics (MVRV Z-Score, NUPL).

Macro: FRED (Federal Reserve) for VIX, T10Y2Y. multpl.com for Shiller CAPE.

All data is public and free.

What does snapshot date 2026-04-24 mean?

The last date through which data is loaded. All backtests end here. If the snapshot is "stale" — that's normal, all numbers on the page correspond to this moment.

To update, you have to re-pull data and recompute the matrix. A one-shot manual operation, not automatic.

What's the test period?

Depends on the market:

  • BTC: stress_b period — from 2017-01-01 to 2026-04-24, ~9.3 years. Covers 2 full halving cycles.
  • ETH: from 2018-01-13 (after the dotcom-style ICO crash of 2017) to 2026-04-24, ~8.3 years.
  • SPY: from 1999-03-10 to 2026-04-24, ~27 years. Includes dotcom, GFC 2008, COVID, 2022.
  • NDX: from 1999-03-10 to 2026-04-24, ~27 years.

How is IRR computed?

Newton's method: solve for the rate r such that the sum of all cashflows (negative deposits, positive terminal value) gives NPV = 0.

Computed at weekly frequency, then raised to the 52nd power for the annual equivalent: IRR_annual = (1 + r_weekly)52 − 1.

Can a backtest like this be trusted?

With caveats — yes. What's honest about it:

  • Only information available at the moment of decision is used (no look-ahead bias)
  • Same engine and logic as in the bot — no ad-hoc optimizations against historical data
  • Metrics computed standardly (Newton IRR, MaxDD from peak as published)
  • Conservation invariant: deposits = spent + idle cash + forgone

What's NOT included: broker fees (typically 0.1-1%), spread (real for crypto), taxes, slippage on large volumes. This will reduce real returns by 1-3% annually. Not a catastrophe, but be aware.

Maybe these strategies were picked by hindsight?

Good question. Data snooping — when you test 1,000 strategies, pick top-3 and say "these are great". In reality it's just noise.

Here, 25 strategies were taken from public ideas (classic DCA, Mayer Multiple, Pi Cycle, MVRV — all from textbooks and crypto blogs). No one calibrated parameters against the current history. Multipliers and thresholds are mostly "round numbers from the spec".

Even so — out-of-sample testing on the next 9 years would show different numbers. Past does NOT predict the future. This is a "what if" study, not a "what will be" forecast.

Practice

Is this financial advice?

No. This is a research project.

I'm not a licensed financial advisor, don't know your circumstances, and bear no responsibility for your decisions. Make your own conclusions. If you plan to invest meaningful money — talk to a real advisor.

How to choose from 25 strategies?

Steps:

  1. Set your risk: what max drawdown can you emotionally tolerate? If 30% is your ceiling, ignore strategies with MaxDD > 30%.
  2. From the remainder, take top-3 by MM (primary metric).
  3. Read each one's description. If you don't understand what the strategy does — don't pick it.
  4. Among finalists, choose the one whose description you can explain to a friend in 30 seconds. Complex strategies don't survive contact with reality.

The site has a "Pick by personality" interactive filter that narrows the choice with 4-5 simple questions.

What's a meaningful DCA timeframe?

Minimum: 6-12 months. Less is no longer DCA, it's market-timing in small steps.

Sweet spot: 2-4 years. On crypto you should at least see one halving cycle. On stocks — survive at least one recession.

If you want long-term: 5-10+ years. Then strategy choice matters less — period length eats the difference between "smart" and "dumb".

What separates real DCA from a simulation?

Main difference: discipline. In a backtest the strategy buys every Friday no matter what. In real life you'll skip a Friday because of work, forget, or be too scared to buy during a crash.

Second: fees and slippage. Each trade loses 0.1-1%, over the long run that eats 1-3% of annual return.

Third: taxes. In most countries selling crypto or stocks is taxable. The backtest computes gross, reality is net.

I don't get any of this. What do I do?

If this is your first DCA — pick B1 (Pure DCA weekly) or B2 (Monthly lump). Just buy a fixed amount on a regular schedule. Don't try to wrap your head around MVRV or Pi Cycle on day one.

"Smart" strategies only work if you understand WHY they're smart. Otherwise you'll miss a signal, fail to react to a drawdown, break everything.

B1 on BTC over 9 years yielded MM ~ 8.5×. Not perfect (B3 lump was 87×), but honest and without emotional swings.

Past performance doesn't predict the future. This is a research project, not financial advice.
